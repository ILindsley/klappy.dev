# How to Read This Book

I was at a low point — the kind where someone handing you a book feels like one more thing you don't have room for. A wise friend understood that. He said, "I know you're not a reader, so there's no obligation to read it. I only ask that you read the first sentence. If that doesn't call your attention, you can stop. But if it does, read the first paragraph. If it doesn't make you want to read any more, don't. If it does, finish the first page."

Repeat for the chapter. Then the section. Then the book.

The book was *The Road Less Traveled* by M. Scott Peck. Its first sentence is three words: "Life is difficult." Those three words either bounce off you or stop you cold, depending on where you are when you read them. Shawn knew that. He didn't need to convince me the book was worth my time. He just needed to get me to the first sentence and let the sentence do its work.

That became the book I've recommended most to others. Second only to the Bible.

I'm asking you the same thing Shawn asked me. Read the first sentence of the preface. If it calls your attention, read the first paragraph. If it doesn't — stop, with my respect.

And if you keep going, don't feel obligated to read straight through. Every chapter opens with a summary. If it speaks to something you're dealing with, dive in. If it doesn't, skip to the next one. I'd rather you read three chapters that matter to you than twenty-one that don't. Your time is worth more than my page count.

---

*For Shawn Pendergrass, who taught me how to hand someone a book.*

## Website Derivative

> For the homepage or essay index, the instruction compresses to a single line:
>
> *"Read the summary. If it calls your attention, dive in. If it doesn't, skip to the next one."*
>
> The Shawn story earns its weight in front matter because you're asking someone to commit to a book. On the homepage, readers are already browsing. They just need permission to browse selectively instead of feeling like they walked into the middle of something and don't know where to start.
