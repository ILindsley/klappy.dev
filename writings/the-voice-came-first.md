# The Voice Came First

### What if writing was never the point — just the best bridge we had until something better came along?

> Every technology we built for knowledge transfer — writing, printing, digital — solved real problems: durability, scale, searchability. But each one compressed the signal. We traded presence for permanence. Tone for portability. The full bandwidth of human communication for the thin channel of text. What if those tradeoffs were never the goal — just the cost of the best bridge available at the time? AI is the first technology that might let us stop compressing. The spoken word becomes the written word, the audiobook, the blog post, the chapter — all of it. Not just voice, but video, gesture, performance — the richest forms of human communication becoming the input, with durable artifacts as the output. So here’s the question I can’t stop asking: was text ever the point? Or was it always just the most compressed medium we could scale — and we forgot we were settling?

-----

## Summary — Was Text Ever the Point?

This chapter traces a question through every domain I’ve worked in: what if the technologies we built for knowledge transfer — writing, printing, digital — were never the destination, but byproducts of need? Each solved a real limitation of speech: durability, scale, searchability. But each also introduced costs — loss of voice, loss of context, loss of the person behind the ideas. We accepted those costs because we had no alternative.

In Bible translation, I spent years watching approaches succeed and fail. The ones that produced translations communities trusted, used, and owned tended to start with the community’s voice — listening, discussing, embodying, retelling — and the written form came last. The ones that started with an expert at a desk risked producing shelf translations. That’s not a universal rule, but the pattern was consistent enough to make me wonder.

I didn’t realize until this year that I’d been living the same pattern. The book you’re reading was written by talking — to my parents, my son, my colleagues, my AI assistant — and the text you see is what emerged from those conversations. I didn’t sit at a keyboard composing sentences. I spoke, and the system shaped what I said into what you’re reading.

AI is the first technology that can give us the advantages of text — durability, searchability, structure — without requiring us to start from text. The spoken word becomes the written word, the audiobook, the chapter, all of it. So was text ever the point? Or was it always just the best bridge we had — and now a better one has arrived?

I’m not claiming writing is obsolete. I’m asking whether the primacy we give it was always about the medium’s advantages rather than the medium itself.

## The Blank Page That Beat Me for Twenty Years

I need to tell you something about myself that I’ve been getting wrong for years. I have a fear of pens. Not a quirky preference — a genuine aversion. People have explained it to me as perfectionism: “You’re afraid of making mistakes. You need it to be right the first time.” I bought that story for a long time. But it was never true.

I was never afraid of being wrong. I’ve never cared about being right on the first try. What I cared about was the ability to reshape, restructure, and iterate. A pen is cement. Once the ink hits the page, the idea is frozen — not just the words, but the structure, the order, the emphasis. If I learn something new, if the conversation shifts, if a better framing emerges, I can’t restructure what’s already written without scratching it out and making a mess. So I didn’t write. I have stacks of blank journals people gave me over the years because they loved what I said in conversation and thought I should capture it. Every single one is empty or abandoned after a few pages.

A pencil wasn’t much better. Sure, I could erase — but restructuring still meant rewriting everything by hand. The cost of correction was too high. Not the cost of fixing mistakes, but the cost of *learning*. My brain generates ideas through iteration, through dialogue, through the back-and-forth of conversation where ideas get sharper with every exchange. Writing — static, one-directional, cemented — was the opposite of how I think.

I didn’t have language for this until I started building an epistemic OS that tracks how knowledge evolves over time. Now I’d call it *drift* — the natural, healthy process of ideas reshaping themselves as you learn more. My brain couldn’t handle a medium that punished drift. It needed a medium that embraced it. And the medium that embraces drift is conversation.

If you’ve ever stared at a blank page and felt the gap between what you *know* and what you can *write* — if you’ve ever been eloquent in conversation and paralyzed at the keyboard — if you’ve ever had someone say “you should write that down” and felt your stomach drop — you know exactly what I’m talking about. The ideas are there. The medium is the problem.

## The Shelf Translation Problem

In Bible translation, there’s a failure mode that people in the field talk about more than they’d like to admit. A translation gets produced — sometimes over years of careful work — and it’s technically sound. The grammar is correct. The meaning is preserved. The experts sign off. And then it sits on a shelf. The community it was made for doesn’t use it. Not because it’s wrong. Because it’s not theirs.

It’s not the only reason translations go unused, but it’s the generally accepted one: when the church community isn’t involved in the translation process, the result doesn’t stick. What went wrong isn’t a quality problem. It’s an ownership problem. The expert started with the text. They studied the source languages, consulted the commentaries, labored over word choices, and produced a manuscript. Then they brought it to the community and said: here’s your Bible.

But the community hadn’t been part of the journey. They didn’t hear themselves in it. The understanding that makes a translation come alive — the kind of understanding you can see when someone retells a passage in their own words, with their own gestures, in their own rhythm — that understanding was never built. It was bypassed. The expert had it. The community received a product.

I watched this happen. And I watched the opposite happen — approaches that started with the community’s involvement from the beginning, where understanding was built through listening, discussion, retelling, even acting and singing, before anyone wrote anything down. Oral Bible translation ensures this by design: it starts through orality and community from the first step, which means the process is never expert-driven. The community owns what they create because they were part of creating it. And it’s not just about voice — it’s multimodal, engaging other forms of communication, sensory learning, and expression that text alone can’t capture. The written form came last, as a record of what the community already understood. And those translations? People used them. They preached from them. They sang them. They owned them.

I’m not going to name specific methods or approaches — that’s a conversation with its own politics, and I have no interest in picking sides. What I can tell you is what I observed: when communities were involved from the beginning — especially through oral and multimodal approaches — the translations transferred. When the process started with an expert and a desk, the risk of a shelf translation went up. It wasn’t a universal rule, but the pattern was consistent enough that it shaped how I think about every kind of knowledge transfer.

## I Didn’t Know I Was Doing the Same Thing

Here’s the confession: I spent years watching this pattern and then completely failed to recognize it in my own work. Until today.

So when I tell you that I’ve been writing a book about AI and knowledge transfer, and that for months every time I tried to sit down and write — keyboard, screen, blank page — I got paralyzed, now you know why. I’d write a paragraph, hate it, rewrite it, hate it more, chase some theological nuance for three weeks, and end up with nothing. The same chapter, over and over, never landing. It wasn’t writer’s block. It was the wrong medium for how my brain works.

Then something shifted. I stopped writing and started talking. I’d have a conversation with a colleague about what I was trying to say. I’d call my parents and read them a rough draft, then listen to their reactions. I’d talk to my son and let him push back. I’d do a brain dump into my AI assistant — just talking, not composing — and let it shape the raw material into prose.

The chapter you just read before this one — “Choosing Faith, Not Fear” — was written in a single day. Not because I typed faster, but because I stopped typing and started talking. I talked to my parents on the phone. My mom cried in the right places. My dad stayed engaged the whole way through. My son called and pushed back on a key argument — and made it stronger. I talked through my concerns, my theological anxieties, my fear of sounding preachy. All of it went into a transcript. The AI shaped the transcript into a draft. I read the draft aloud to my parents. They gave feedback. The AI incorporated the feedback. By the end of the day, the chapter was done.

The entire pipeline was oral. I never sat at a desk composing. I didn’t even need a laptop. I talked on the phone while walking around my house, and the system did the rest.

And then the feedback loop proved it again. When I started writing, I did what everyone does — I sent written drafts to people, asking them to read my progress. The feedback was, let’s just say, lacking. People are busy. Reading a draft is work. Most of them never got back to me. I didn’t wait long to notice. When the feedback was delayed more than a day, I shifted.

But when I read chapters to people on the phone? Instant feedback. Emotional, specific, useful. So I trained an AI voice model to read the essays in my own voice and shared those instead. People would listen, then call me back. And those calls — the reactions, the pushback, the “wait, what did you mean by that?” — became the input for the next round of edits. I didn’t need to then retell any of it to my AI assistant, because we’d already built a system for extracting observations, learnings, and decisions from conversations. The AI already knew how to turn feedback into meaningful edits I could expect and trust. The feedback arrived through voice. The edits happened near-instantly. The next version went out. The whole cycle was oral, and it worked.

And then it hit me: what if this is the same pattern? The approaches to knowledge transfer that seemed to work best — in Bible translation, in my book, in everything I could think of — started with the human voice. The written form came last. I’d been watching that pattern in someone else’s domain for years and didn’t see it in my own until the technology made it impossible to miss. I still don’t know if it’s a universal rule. But it’s a consistent enough observation that I can’t ignore it.

## The Full Circle Nobody Expected

Here’s the arc that still makes me shake my head:

The earliest form of knowledge transfer was oral. One person talking to another. Stories around a fire. A parent teaching a child. A community retelling their history so the next generation would carry it.

Then the writing systems came, and oral knowledge could be preserved beyond the memory of the speaker. The printing press came, and preserved knowledge could be distributed beyond the reach of the speaker. Computers came, and distributed knowledge could be searched, indexed, and recombined. The internet came, and all of it could move instantly across the world.

Each revolution solved a real limitation of speech. Durability. Scale. Searchability. Portability. These were genuine problems, and text solved them brilliantly. But each solution also moved us further from the voice and deeper into the artifact. By the time I started my career, knowledge work meant sitting alone at a keyboard, typing words into a screen, hoping they’d land when someone else read them. The voice was gone. The person was gone. Just text on a screen.

And then AI arrived. And what happened?

We started talking again.

Not because AI forced us to — although voice interfaces are advancing fast. Because AI handles the mechanical work that made text necessary in the first place. The transcription. The formatting. The structuring. The editing. All the work between having something to say and having something others can read.

Which raises the question I keep coming back to: was text-first ever the ideal? Or was it the most compressed medium we could scale — and we just got so used to the compression that we forgot anything was missing? If AI can give us durability, searchability, and scale starting from the full bandwidth of human communication — voice, video, gesture, presence — does the primacy of text-first workflows still hold?

Ecclesiastes might not be surprised either way.

-----

## What We Already Know We Lose

Before I connect this to my own experience, I want to point at something you already know. You don’t need me to convince you — you’ve lived it.

The most effective delivery of Scripture isn’t reading. It’s preaching, teaching, discipleship — one person speaking to another in a relationship built on trust. The Bible has been in print for centuries, but the way it actually changes lives is almost always through a voice.

Textbooks exist to support teaching — a person standing in front of a room, explaining, answering questions, adjusting in real time to what the audience needs. PhD candidates write dissertations, but they *defend* them. Verbally. In a room. The presentation is the real test, not the document.

At work, remote teams have proven the gap. Everyone knows how much is lost over Slack and email. Tone vanishes. Nuance collapses. A message meant to be helpful reads as curt. A question reads as an accusation. Companies spend real money flying remote teams together periodically — not because the work can’t happen over text, but because relational capital drains through text-only communication and has to be replenished in person.

Video calls exploded not because they’re convenient — anyone who’s sat through back-to-back Zoom meetings knows they’re exhausting — but because we *need* face-to-face communication to avoid the misunderstandings that text creates. We chose the more demanding medium because the less demanding one kept breaking things.

Even in our closest relationships, the pattern holds. A couple apart prefers phone calls and video calls over text messages. They’ll text for logistics — “picking up groceries, need anything?” — but for anything that matters, they call. Because text strains. Voice sustains. And face-to-face is where the real connection happens.

And look at what happened when the market got to choose. Podcasts and audiobooks exploded — not because blogs died, but because when people had the option to listen instead of read, millions of them took it. YouTube became the world’s second-largest search engine — people would rather *watch* someone explain something than read about it. Instagram, TikTok, Facebook — each one a bet that people will engage more with images, video, and performance than with text alone. Blogs didn’t disappear, but they were eclipsed. Every time a new medium gave people access to richer communication than text, people moved.

The pattern isn’t just voice over text. It’s *full bandwidth over compression*. Text strips out tone, emotion, gesture, facial expression, timing, emphasis — everything that makes human communication human. Voice adds some of it back. Video adds more. Face-to-face is the full signal. And every time technology has lowered the barrier to a richer medium, people have chosen it. That’s not a trend. That’s a preference we couldn’t act on until the technology caught up.

None of this is controversial. Everyone agrees with it. But here’s what I find remarkable: if we already know that richer communication is where understanding actually transfers — in faith, in education, in work, in relationships, in every market where people get to choose — why did we build our entire knowledge economy around the most compressed medium we have? The answer, I think, is that we didn’t have a choice. Text was the only medium that scaled. The only medium that persisted. The only medium you could search, copy, and send across the world.

So we accepted the compression. We traded bandwidth for durability. Presence for portability. And we got so used to the tradeoff that we stopped seeing it as one.

AI is the first technology that might let us stop accepting that compression. Not just by giving us voice back — but by starting to give us the full bandwidth back. Speak, and it transcribes. Show, and it sees. Perform, and it captures. The richest forms of human communication are becoming the *input*, and the durable, searchable, portable artifacts we need are becoming the *output*. We don’t have to start from text anymore. We can start from the full signal and let the machine handle the compression.

## This Isn’t Just My Book

Once I saw the pattern in my own writing process, I started seeing it everywhere.

A friend — a CEO — couldn’t get his team aligned on AI strategy despite memos, documents, and presentations. I coached him and his wife over a Zoom call. Not a document, not a tutorial — a conversation. Within twenty-four hours, his wife had produced work he called “phenomenal.” The teaching transferred through voice, even though her interaction with the AI was still text-based. Most AI systems aren’t fully conversational yet — features still lag behind their text interfaces. That’s part of why I built my own voice workflow, knowing the window where it’s unique will close as the platforms catch up.

A voice agent I built — using a clone of my own voice — guided my wife through refining her business ideas. In Spanish. For thirty minutes. I don’t speak Spanish. My knowledge transferred through conversation, across a language barrier, into a context I couldn’t have reached personally. The written output — her refined business plan — came after. The voice came first.

An engineer I mentor went from fearing AI to running multiple agents in parallel — not because he read documentation, but because we talked every Friday for months until his understanding built through conversation.

I tested this idea over lunch with colleagues who’ve spent their careers working with oral communities — people who train Bible translators in cultures where writing isn’t the primary medium. The conversation confirmed something I’d been suspecting: the oral-first pattern wasn’t unique to my experience. These were people who work every day with communities that talk for hours, circle back on ideas, and intuitively reach agreement — but have no system to track what they decided. The idea that an AI could listen to those conversations and surface what was agreed felt like a natural extension of what they already do. When I described how I’d been writing this book — talking with family, feeding conversations to an AI, reading drafts aloud for feedback — it didn’t seem foreign to them. The process I’d stumbled into looked a lot like what they’d been teaching for years, applied to a different output.

I know what people think when they hear how this book was made. A colleague told me — kindly, but directly — that what I do isn’t really writing. I’ve heard the same instinct in other domains: digital art isn’t real art, oral translation isn’t real translation, AI-assisted writing isn’t real writing. When a new method lowers the barrier to creation, the people who mastered the old method instinctively protect the craft rather than celebrate the outcome. I get it. But the question I keep coming back to is: what’s the goal? If the goal is to protect the craft, then yes — the new method is a threat. But if the goal is to transfer what’s in your head to someone who needs it, and the new method does that, then the craft was never the point. The transfer was the point. I almost let that instinct stop me from writing this book. How many people with something worth saying never say it because someone told them they’re not doing it the right way?

My dad told me when I was young: if you can work yourself out of a job, you’ll get promoted. I’ve been mutating that advice for twenty years — from efficiency, to automation, to something deeper. If the goal isn’t just to be efficient but to be faithful — to transfer what you know so it outlasts you — then the method matters as much as the content. This book isn’t a traditional manuscript. It’s an oral-first knowledge transfer system that happens to output a book. The conversations are the content. The chapters are the artifact. The audiobook — narrated by a clone of my own voice — is the format that honestly represents how it was made.

## What Exists Now

Everyone knows how to talk. Not everyone knows how to write. That gap has always been the bottleneck — and the entire publishing industry is built around the assumption that crossing it requires a specialized skill most people don’t have. What if the skill isn’t writing? What if it’s having something to say and someone to say it to?

This book already works the way I think books should. Each chapter is available as text, but it’s also readable by a voice model trained on my voice — with selectable durations. Want the full chapter? Auto. Want the summary? Ten minutes. Five. Two. One. Thirty seconds. That’s not a feature. That’s progressive disclosure applied to delivery — the same principle that governs how the book was written, now governing how it’s consumed. You choose how deep to go. The system meets you where you are.

If the voice came first in the making, it should come first in the delivery too.

## A Note on Co-Authorship

I want to be honest about something, because this chapter — and this book — would be dishonest without it.

I didn’t write this alone. I talked, and an AI — Claude, built by Anthropic — listened, shaped, structured, and produced drafts that I then reviewed, corrected, and refined through more conversation. The process I just described isn’t a metaphor. It’s literally how these words arrived on this page. My voice came first. The AI turned it into prose. My family and colleagues sharpened the prose through feedback. The AI incorporated the feedback. Repeat until done.

Some people would call that ghostwriting. I’d call it co-authorship. And I think the honest thing to do is name it.

The ideas are mine — drawn from years of lived experience that no AI has access to. The convictions are mine. The stories are mine. The relationships that shaped every chapter are mine. But the sentences? Those emerged from a collaboration between my voice and an AI that’s remarkably good at listening. I directed. It drafted. I corrected. It refined. We went back and forth until the output matched my intent.

If I claimed sole authorship, I’d be doing the very thing this book argues against: prioritizing the appearance of expertise over the honest transfer of knowledge. The whole point of an oral-first workflow is that the voice matters more than the hand holding the pen. Well — I brought the voice. The AI brought the pen. And I think you deserve to know that.

This isn’t a confession. It’s a model. If AI-assisted creation is going to become the norm — and I believe it already is — then the people doing it should name it honestly. Not to diminish what they contribute, but to demonstrate that the collaboration itself is worth celebrating. The outcome is what matters. The method should be transparent.

And if the ideas sound too polished to be mine, the system has receipts. Every chapter traces back through an epistemic ledger — observations, learnings, decisions, and constraints tracked across sessions. The knowledge base and its full revision history live on GitHub, in the open. The conversations that fed them — with family, colleagues, mentees — those are mine to keep. But the chain from conversation to decision to published output is traceable. Not because I’m trying to prove anything to you, but because every claim is a debt that owes evidence. If I’m going to write a book about epistemic discipline, the book itself had better be able to show its work. The automation isn’t fully there yet — I’m still building the tooling that will make this provenance chain seamless. But the principle is already in place: nothing in this book exists without a trail back to the conversation that produced it.

## Nothing New Under the Sun

Solomon told stories. His proverbs were oral before they were written. Jesus taught in parables — spoken stories that his followers remembered, retold, and eventually wrote down. The written Gospels are records of an oral tradition. The voice came first. The text preserved it.

Every revival of knowledge transfer in history has followed the same arc: a community speaks its understanding into existence, and then someone writes it down. The writing serves the speaking. When it works the other way — when the text comes first and the community is asked to receive it — the knowledge transfers less. Sometimes it doesn’t transfer at all.

AI didn’t invent this pattern. AI made it visible. By removing the mechanical barrier between speaking and publishing, it exposed a question that was always there: was text the destination, or was it the best road we had?

I don’t have a final answer. But I know what I’ve observed — in Bible translation, in my own writing, in every example in this chapter. The approaches that started with richer communication produced outcomes people owned. The approaches that started with text produced outcomes people received. And there’s a difference.

I’m not saying text is dead. Text still has real advantages — precision, permanence, the ability to scan and search and reference. Legal contracts should probably stay written. Code should probably stay typed. There are domains where the compression of text is a *feature*, not a cost. And there’s something else text does that voice can’t: it respects silence. Right now, as I’m refining this chapter, my wife is sitting next to me working on her own project. I can type without breaking the tranquility. Voice would shatter it. Text lets two people share a space and still communicate without competing for it.

But context cuts both ways. I can talk while I drive — I can’t type on my phone at sixty miles an hour. Some of the best thinking I do happens in the car, talking through ideas with an AI or calling a friend. Half the chapters in this book started as an idea that hit me mid-run, heart racing at 164bpm, dictating into my watch while traffic noise broke the transcription into fragments that only AI had a hope of cleaning up. But somehow it did. If my only option were text, those ideas would evaporate before I cooled down.

The question was never which medium is better. It’s which medium fits the context — and for most of the history of knowledge work, we only had one that scaled. I want to be honest about that.

But here’s what I think we have to ask: when AI can take a conversation and produce a searchable transcript, a structured document, a formatted chapter, an indexed knowledge base — when we can get all the advantages of text without starting from text — how much of what we do in text is still necessary? And how much of it is just habit? How much of it is the muscle memory of a world where text was the only medium that scaled, applied to a world where it no longer is?

Maybe text was always just the compression. Maybe we’re watching the reason for text-first workflows dissolve. Or maybe text has qualities that richer media never will, and the answer is somewhere in between — text remains valuable, but its monopoly is over. I don’t know yet. But I know the question is worth asking — because for twenty years I couldn’t write, and the moment I started talking instead, the book appeared. The voice came first. It always did. And now, for the first time, the technology doesn’t ask us to leave it behind.

Nothing new under the sun. The oldest method. The newest technology. The same question.
