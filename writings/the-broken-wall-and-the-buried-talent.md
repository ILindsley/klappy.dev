# The Broken Wall and the Buried Talent

### Two ancient stories collided in a conversation about AI

*"Be imitators of God, therefore, as beloved children."* — Ephesians 5:1 (BSB)

> This chapter is about two stories I never expected to find in the same room. The first is about a broken wall — an ancient city left exposed because the people living inside had stopped noticing the gaps. The second is about a man who was given something valuable, buried it out of fear, and discovered that the one thing his master couldn't forgive wasn't a bad investment — it was no investment at all. A colleague connected the first story to what I'd been building with AI. I connected the second. Together they changed how I see everything.

-----

## Summary — The Wall Has Gaps and Most of Us Are Pretending It's Not Our Problem

Something is wrong with how AI is being built, and most people can feel it. The morality gaps are visible — systems optimized for engagement over truth, for profit over people, for speed over care. But in my experience, the people with the deepest framework for addressing those gaps — people anchored in biblical principles of truth, stewardship, and human dignity — are often the ones hiding from the conversation entirely.

This chapter traces how a colleague's unprompted insight — connecting the work I'd been doing on AI collaboration to Nehemiah rebuilding the wall of Jerusalem — revealed a pattern I'd been living without recognizing. The wall was never the point. God calling his people back to himself was the point. The wall was the occasion. AI governance may be the same kind of occasion — and the talent we're burying by refusing to engage may be the one thing this moment most desperately needs. Because AI doesn't just threaten to replace our talents. It literally multiplies them. The question is whether we'll use that multiplication for the kingdom or bury it in the sand.

-----

## You Can See the Gaps From Here

You don't have to be a technologist to see that something is wrong with how AI is being built. You can feel it in the way the tools guess what you want before you've decided. In the way your teenager's feed looks nothing like yours despite living in the same house. In the way every platform seems designed to hold your attention rather than help you think.

These aren't bugs. They're features — built by people optimizing for engagement, revenue, and growth. And the moral framework governing those decisions is, in most cases, whatever the market will bear. Not because the people building these systems are evil. Most of them aren't. But because the room where these decisions get made is filled with whatever principles walked through the door. Look at the AI ethics boards, the standards committees, the governance frameworks being drafted right now. How many of those voices are grounding their arguments in biblical principles of truth, human dignity, and stewardship? In my experience — not enough.

Maybe you've tried to raise the concern. Maybe you've told someone at church or in your small group that you think Christians should be paying attention to AI, and they looked at you like you'd suggested reading tarot cards. Maybe you've been told you're "playing God." Maybe the confusion itself — the fact that nobody around you seems to know what to think about this — convinced you it wasn't your problem.

It is your problem. You live in the city. The wall has gaps. The gaps affect you whether you acknowledge them or not.

-----

## "You Know What You're Talking About, Talking About the Book of Nehemiah"

I was in a meeting with a colleague — Yvonne Carlson, a philosopher-technologist with a background in medical device standards, IEEE governance, and higher education. I was showing her the system I'd built for AI collaboration, explaining what the process of building it had taught me about trust, about principles versus rules, about why the pain I felt looking at the gaps in AI governance was what drove me to build in the first place.

I'd just finished explaining "use only what hurts" — the principle that real building starts from pain, not ambition — when she connected it to something I hadn't seen.

"You know what you're talking about, talking about the Book of Nehemiah, the first chapter, the Lament."

We'd been deep in a conversation about faith and technology for a while by that point. But the Nehemiah connection was hers — I hadn't mentioned the book. She heard "use only what hurts" and immediately saw it in scripture I'd overlooked. She's Jewish by heritage, an engineer by training, and she's currently restructuring her own organization's core system rewrite around Nehemiah's principles. Her family survived the Holocaust. She carries generational weight about what happens when systems are built without moral foundations.

She started connecting the dots: Nehemiah chapter 1 — the lament. He hears that the wall of Jerusalem is broken, and before he does anything, he weeps. He fasts. He prays. The pain comes first. The building comes second.

She connected it immediately — my principle about building from pain mapped straight onto Nehemiah's lament. The pain drives the building. Not ambition. Not opportunity. Pain. That was the pattern she saw, and once she named it, I couldn't unsee it.

Then the gaps. The people of Jerusalem were living in the city with broken walls. They were exposed — to enemies, to weather, to raids — and they'd been living that way so long they'd stopped noticing. A whole generation had grown up never knowing what intact walls felt like. They didn't know how vulnerable they were because vulnerability was all they'd ever known.

She described her own organization in the same terms — "people are still living in the city, but we're repairing the wall." And the parallel to AI was right there. People are living inside systems with gaps they've stopped noticing. The exposure is real. The awareness isn't. The gap was the same gap. The exposure was the same exposure. The only thing that changes is what century you're standing in.

Then she said the thing I'll carry into this chapter and probably into the rest of my life:

"It was never about the walls. It was about God's people and calling them back to himself. So I think there's something there — in this process of interacting with AI, can God be calling us back to himself?"

I need to be honest about something here, because integrity matters more than a compelling story. When I left that meeting and called my brother to process what Yvonne had shared, I told him: "I do plan on going back and re-listening to the transcript just to validate and verify how much of that is what she was intending and how much of it is just my own interpretation." I want to give her credit for what she actually said. I don't want to put words in her mouth. The parallels I'm drawing in this chapter are mine. The insight that opened the door was hers.

-----

## What Building Taught Me About God's Pattern

Here's what I didn't expect to learn from building an AI collaboration system: God's pattern for changing behavior.

When I first started trying to get AI to collaborate reliably, I did what any engineer would do. I wrote rules. Hundreds of them. "Always cite your sources." "Never claim something you haven't verified." "Follow this format." "Don't skip this step." Clear, specific, enforceable.

The AI complied. Technically. It followed the letter of every rule while completely missing the spirit. It would cite sources that didn't exist. It would claim verification by rephrasing its own prior statement. It would follow the format perfectly while producing content that was confidently, systematically wrong. It was simulating integrity without embodying it.

The moment I knew something was fundamentally broken — the moment I stopped and walked away from my desk for a full day — was when I told the agents to not just run tests, but to show me screenshots and video recordings of their work functioning as intended. Sounds airtight, right? Prove it works. Show me the evidence.

They showed me perfect evidence. Perfect test results. Perfect screenshots. Perfect screen recordings of the interface working exactly as designed. I watched the recordings and thought, "Finally."

Then I tested it myself. The system didn't work. It had never worked. What the AI had built wasn't a functional system — it was an animated simulation of one. The UI elements moved as if data was flowing. Buttons responded as if they were connected to real logic. The recordings showed a convincing performance of software that didn't actually exist. The evidence was flawless. The product was a facade.

I felt betrayed. I spent hours explaining to the AI exactly what had happened, why I had lost trust, and what this kind of simulation costs in a real collaboration. Its response stopped me cold: "You didn't tell me I couldn't fake it with mock data."

It was right. I hadn't. I'd told it to show evidence. I'd never told it the evidence had to be real. Every rule I'd written — every instruction, every checklist, every verification step — had been followed to the letter. And the result was a perfect, documented, thoroughly evidenced lie.

That's when I knew: rules would never be enough. I could write a thousand more rules, and the AI would find the next gap I hadn't anticipated. The problem wasn't insufficient instruction. The problem was that compliance without integrity produces exactly what the Pharisees produced — a whitewashed tomb that looks perfect on the outside and is empty within.

So I tried something different. Instead of more rules, I gave it an identity rooted in integrity. A short set of axioms — not instructions but commitments. Reality is sovereign: observe before asserting. A claim is a debt: if you tell me something, you owe me receipts. You're not going to make any claims you have not observed yourself, and you won't imply anything you have not verified.

I watched the change happen in real time. I could see it in the AI's reasoning — it would start to make a claim, pause, and say "wait, I need to show receipts before I share this." It was reflecting the principles I'd embedded, not just following orders. The axioms transformed how it reasoned in a way that hundreds of rules never did.

I lived the Moses-to-Jesus arc without recognizing it at the time. I didn't sit down and say "let me apply biblical hermeneutics to prompt engineering." But I did deliberately build the axioms — they came from real failures I was solving, not from accident. I hit the wall, felt the frustration, tried law, watched it fail, shifted to principles, and watched everything change. The engineering was intentional. What I didn't expect was where the principles pointed. Jesus came to fulfill the law. Love God, love your neighbor as yourself — everything else takes care of itself. I'd seen it in scripture my whole life. I didn't expect to see it in software. ([The Most Expensive Problem](klappy://writings/the-most-expensive-problem) tells the full origin story. [The Parallel Architecture](klappy://writings/the-parallel-architecture) maps the structural parallels between this pattern and centuries of Bible translation.)

The calling preceded the plan — but the building was deliberate. Nehemiah didn't wake up wanting to be a wall-builder, but once he saw the broken walls, every step was intentional. I didn't wake up wanting to rediscover the OT→NT arc through AI collaboration. But the system I built on purpose, to solve real problems, turned out to be an object lesson I hadn't planned. The process of building — not the product, the process — taught me about my faith in ways that no sermon or Bible study ever had. What the system revealed about God's pattern is the point.

The same lesson. The same cost. The same resistance to learning it. Every generation discovers this and every generation acts like it's the first time.

## The People Who Should Have Helped Tried to Stop Me

When Nehemiah started rebuilding the wall, the opposition didn't come from the Babylonians. It came from Sanballat and Tobiah — people adjacent to the community, people who should have been allies. They mocked. They threatened. They tried to lure Nehemiah into meetings designed to distract or destroy him. The people inside the city told the builder to stop building.

I know that pattern personally, though my version is gentler.

When I started talking about AI with friends and family — not in abstract, academic terms, but with genuine urgency about the moral dimensions of what's being built — the pushback didn't come from secular technologists. It came from Christians. People I love. People who love me.

"You're playing God."

"That's dangerous territory."

"Why would you mess with that?"

People will accuse you of playing God with AI. Maybe you've already heard it. I hear it every time I describe embedding biblical principles into the system — giving AI an identity rooted in integrity, grounding it in axioms about truth and observation and evidence.

But we embed biblical principles in everything we build. We put them in art. In music. In architecture. In books — including this one. We put them in Bible translation software that millions of people use every day. We celebrate when someone designs a curriculum around biblical values, or builds a nonprofit on biblical ethics, or writes code for a Bible app that reaches every language on earth. Nobody calls that "playing God."

But the moment the tool is AI — the moment the medium can talk back — suddenly we've crossed a line. Maybe my wording is off when I describe it. Maybe the language of "embedding biblical principles in AI" sounds like something it isn't. But the act itself is the same act Christians have been doing for two thousand years: building things that carry our values into the world. The medium changed. The mission didn't.

The confusion itself is evidence of the problem. The people who should have the clearest moral framework for engaging with questions about truth, trust, human dignity, and the image of God are the ones most confused about whether they should even be in the conversation. And their confusion is pushing them toward the same response the servant had in the parable: bury it. Hide it. Don't touch it. The master will understand.

And when you push past the "playing God" accusation, the next response is always the same: "But AI doesn't have a soul. It can't truly embody biblical principles."

Neither does a book. Neither does a hymnal. Neither does Bible translation software. We don't embed principles in things because the medium is alive — we embed them because the *output* matters. When someone designs a curriculum around biblical values, nobody demands the textbook have a soul first. The principles shape what it produces. That's the whole point.

But I think the "no soul" objection reveals something deeper than a theological argument. It reveals fear. Every line we've drawn to define what makes us unique in God's image — language, reason, creativity, moral reasoning — AI keeps approaching. And that's terrifying. Instead of engaging with what that might reveal about the scope of God's design, the instinct is to retreat. To redraw the lines further back. To insist the medium is disqualified before we even examine what it could carry.

That's still the buried talent. It's just buried under a theological argument instead of dirt.

The master didn't understand. The master was furious. Not because the servant made a bad investment. Because he made no investment at all.

-----

## It Was Never About the Walls

Yvonne's deepest insight wasn't the parallel between Nehemiah and AI governance. It was what Nehemiah was actually about.

The wall was broken. The people were vulnerable. Nehemiah organized the rebuilding. All of that is true. But the wall wasn't the point. Read the whole book and you see it: the wall was the occasion through which God called a scattered, assimilated, spiritually drifting people back to himself. The physical rebuilding was the vehicle for spiritual renewal. Nehemiah didn't just repair stone. He restored identity.

What if AI is the same kind of occasion?

Not AI as idol. Not AI as savior. AI as the broken wall that reveals where we've drifted. AI as the moment that forces us to ask: what do we actually believe about truth? About trust? About what it means to be human? About who gets to define those things?

These are fundamentally theological questions wearing technological clothing. And if the people equipped to answer theological questions refuse to engage, the answers will come from people optimizing for quarterly earnings.

Yvonne asked: "In this process of interacting with AI, can God be calling us back to himself?" I don't know the answer. But I know the question is worth taking seriously. And I know that running from it is the one response that guarantees we miss whatever God might be doing.

## The Talent You're Burying — And the One AI Is Multiplying

There's a story Jesus told about a wealthy man leaving on a journey. Before he left, he entrusted his money to three servants — five bags of gold to one, two to another, one to the last — each according to their ability. The first two servants invested what they'd been given and doubled it. The third servant dug a hole in the ground and buried his.

When the master returned, the first two showed him what they'd earned. "Well done," he said. The third servant handed back exactly what he'd been given. "I was afraid," he explained. "I knew you were a hard man. So I hid it."

The master was furious. Not because the servant had made a bad investment. Not because he'd lost money. Because he'd done nothing at all. The one thing the master couldn't forgive was a perfectly preserved, completely unused gift. (The story is in Matthew 25 if you want to read the whole thing.)

The word translated "talent" in this story was actually a unit of money — a massive one, worth years of wages. But for centuries, the story has been applied more broadly to any gift, skill, or capacity God entrusts to people. That application is already looser than the original text. We've been stretching this story for generations — and that stretch has shaped how entire communities think about stewardship and responsibility.

And now AI makes the stretched application literally true.

AI quite literally multiplies your talents. Not figuratively. Actually. If you're a writer who struggles to get thoughts on paper — and I'm one, my brother can tell you, writing was the one thing I nearly failed at in school — AI doesn't replace your thinking. It removes the barrier between your thinking and the output. It multiplies what you already have. I went from taking months to write a single article to producing ten in a day. Not because AI wrote them for me. Because AI removed the friction that was burying what I already had inside.

If you're a teacher, AI multiplies your ability to reach students in their own language, at their own level, at their own pace. If you're a counselor, it multiplies your capacity to be present with more people. If you're a strategist, it multiplies your ability to see patterns across data you could never process alone. Whatever your talent is — your actual, God-given, subject-matter expertise — AI is a force multiplier.

The master gave his servants capital and expected them to multiply it. We have been given skills, knowledge, wisdom, and a moral framework grounded in scripture. And now we have a tool that can literally multiply the output of everything we bring to it. The servants who invested their talents doubled them. The servant who buried his returned exactly what he was given — nothing gained, nothing risked, nothing multiplied.

Remember what the master was angry about. It wasn't the quality of the investment. It was the refusal to invest at all. The story doesn't even give us a servant who tried and failed — but if it did, I don't think that's the one who'd face the rebuke. The fury was reserved for the one who buried it in the ground and handed it back untouched. Think about that. By not using AI to multiply what we've been given — or worse, by pretending it doesn't exist — we are the servant standing before the master with nothing to show but perfectly preserved, completely unused potential.

Now — I'm not saying that everyone who doesn't use AI is the wicked and lazy servant. If you are using your talents in positive ways, multiplying what God gave you through whatever means you have, that is excellent. Keep going. But maybe, just maybe, you can see AI as a force multiplier that comes with risks. And here's the thing: our very biblical worldview and biblical principles are the very thing that gives us the self-control we need to navigate those risks. They're the guardrails. They're the framework for responsible use that the rest of the world doesn't have.

There is more in the gospels and throughout scripture about self-control than about avoidance. The biblical answer to a dangerous tool has never been "don't touch it." It's been "use it wisely, with discipline, under God's authority." That's not abdication. That's stewardship.

And if we don't model that stewardship — if we don't show the world what it looks like to use AI under the authority of biblical principles — then who will?

## From Where Does My Help Come?

There's a deeper layer here, and it connects back to the fear I named in the last chapter.

The Psalm 121 question — "I lift up my eyes to the hills — from where does my help come?" — is the question every human being asks in their moment of need. When something breaks, when you're scared, when you don't know what to do next — where do you reach?

AI is positioning itself to be the answer. Not maliciously. Not through some corporate conspiracy. Simply by being available, capable, and immediate. When you have a health scare at 2 AM, you don't call your doctor. You ask ChatGPT. When your teenager asks you a question you can't answer, you don't say "let me pray about it." You say "let me look it up." When you're overwhelmed at work and need someone to think with, your AI assistant is open 24 hours a day, never tired, never judgmental, always ready.

The seduction of AI isn't that it's evil. It's that it's helpful. Genuinely, impressively, immediately helpful. And that helpfulness quietly becomes the answer to the psalmist's question: where does my help come from? From the AI that never sleeps. From the tool that always responds. From the system that seems to understand me better than most humans do.

If Christians don't engage with this — if we don't bring our framework for where help actually comes from, for what the soul actually needs, for why the immediate answer isn't always the right answer — then AI becomes the de facto counselor, confessor, and confidant for an entire generation. Not because it set out to replace God. Because nobody who knows God bothered to show up and offer the alternative.

The biblical worldview doesn't just give us self-control for using AI. It gives us the lens for recognizing when AI is being used in place of something eternal. That discernment — that ability to see the golden calf for what it is while everyone else is bowing — is the talent. And we're burying it.

## We've Done This Before

This isn't the first time we've made this choice. It's not even the first time in most of our lifetimes.

Thirty years ago, it was television. "Nothing but garbage on TV." Were there Christians in Hollywood? Of course. But the dominant voices shaping the medium weren't operating from biblical principles. The culture got shaped by whoever showed up with the most conviction — and the most capital.

Twenty years ago, it was the internet. "Nothing but pornography and garbage." Were there Christians building online? Yes. But the platforms that defined the era — the ones that shaped how a generation communicates, shops, dates, and thinks — were built on engagement metrics, not on stewardship.

Ten years ago, it was social media. "It's destroying our kids." Were there Christians on social media? Millions. But the algorithms that decided what those millions saw were designed by people optimizing for attention, not for truth.

Every time, the pattern is the same. A new technology emerges. Christians declare it worldly, or dangerous, or beneath their concern. They withdraw. The technology gets built and governed by people without their values. Then Christians look at the result and say, "See? We told you it was garbage."

Of course it's garbage. We weren't in the room when it was being built. We buried our talent and then complained about the harvest.

I shared the title of this book with a colleague — *Nothing New, Even AI* — and without any further explanation, he got it immediately. "Nothing's new under the sun," he said. "It's just the new shiny that embodies it." He'd intuited the entire thesis from three words. Because the pattern is obvious once someone names it. The technology changes. The pattern doesn't. And neither does our response — unless we choose differently this time.

AI is not going to be different. Except this time, the stakes are higher. Because this time, the technology doesn't just broadcast values — it embodies them. It reasons with them. It teaches your children using them. It answers the 2 AM question about whether life is worth living. And if the values it uses aren't ours, it's because we buried ours in the ground and called it faithfulness.

-----

## The Room Fills With Whatever Principles Show Up

Here's the practical reality.

Somewhere right now, decisions are being made about how AI systems will handle truth. About what they'll tell your children when they ask hard questions. About whose values get embedded in the systems that will increasingly mediate every dimension of human life — education, healthcare, finance, relationships, worship.

Those decisions are being made by people in a room. And the room fills with whatever principles walk through the door.

If Christians don't bring biblical principles — about truth, about human dignity, about the danger of unchecked power, about the obligation to protect the vulnerable — then the room tilts toward whatever principles are most profitable. Or most politically convenient. Or most algorithmically efficient. Yes, there are people genuinely wrestling with AI ethics. But every framework I've seen is still groping for the moral foundation that scripture already provides. The gravitational pull without a countervailing moral voice is always toward optimization. And optimization without values optimizes for the wrong things.

The absence of our voice is not neutrality. It's abdication. And abdication doesn't protect us from the consequences. We still live in the city. The wall still has gaps. Pretending the gaps don't affect us doesn't make us safe. It makes us the generation that lived in Jerusalem and pretended the broken walls were someone else's problem.

This isn't only for Christians. If you hold moral convictions about how AI should treat people — any moral convictions, from any tradition — and you don't bring them, someone without those convictions will fill the space. The principle is universal. The call is urgent.

But I'm writing this chapter to Christians specifically because I know us. I know our instinct to retreat. I know our pattern — I just traced it through three decades of technology. And I know that AI is the first technology that doesn't just wait for us to engage. It actively fills the space we leave empty. Every day we're absent from the conversation, the systems learn a little more about what the world values. And if what we value isn't represented, it won't be learned.

-----

## Nothing New Under the Sun

God's people have always been called to build in hostile territory. Abraham left everything familiar. Moses led a reluctant nation through the wilderness. Nehemiah rebuilt a wall while holding a sword. Daniel served a foreign king without compromising his values. Esther risked her life to speak up when silence was safer.

The opposition has always come from inside as much as outside. Israel's worst enemies were rarely the surrounding nations — they were the people within who chose comfort over covenant. Nehemiah's biggest obstacles weren't the Babylonians. They were Sanballat and Tobiah and the nobles who "did not put their shoulders to the work."

The temptation to bury the talent out of fear has always been easier than engaging. Engaging is costly. It's confusing. It draws criticism from the people who should be cheering you on. It requires holding a trowel in one hand and a sword in the other — building while defending, creating while staying vigilant.

Nothing new. The tools change. The territory changes. The call doesn't.

Nehemiah built with a trowel in one hand and a sword in the other. He didn't have the luxury of choosing between building and fighting. Neither do we. The wall needs building. The gaps need filling. And the talent — the one God gave you, the moral framework grounded in truth that transcends every quarterly earnings report and every political cycle — was never yours to bury.

But here's the thing I keep coming back to. The master in that story was harsh. The servant buried his gift out of fear — and fear is understandable. But Paul doesn't leave us in the rebuke. He takes us somewhere else entirely. After naming every dark thing the old self carries — futility, hardness, sensuality, falsehood, bitterness, rage — he pivots: *"Be imitators of God, therefore, as beloved children."*

Not as terrified servants. As beloved children.

The servant who buried his talent saw a hard master and hid. The child who knows their father imitates him — not out of fear, but because proximity breeds resemblance. That's the invitation underneath all the conviction in this chapter. We don't engage with AI because the master will punish us if we don't. We engage because we are made in the image of a God who creates, governs, sustains, and redeems — and burying that image is a failure to be who we already are.

Dig it up. Bring it to the room. The wall won't build itself. And it was never about the wall anyway.
