# Shifting Bottlenecks, Climbing Ladders: The Real Game in AI-Augmented Work

> The bottleneck in AI-augmented work never disappears. It moves along two axes: a pendulum between operator and agent, and a ladder of delegation maturity on the operator side. A year ago the bottleneck was my hands. This week it was my thumb. The model got me to the next rung. My management maturity decides whether I can stand on it. And the micro-rituals so small they don't register as friction (swipe, tap, continue, swipe, tap, continue) are the ones that absorb me into the workflow before I notice they exist.

---

## Summary/TL;DR — The Bottleneck Moves. The Ladder Is Real. The Rituals Are the Smell.

The bottleneck in AI-augmented work never goes away. It moves. It swings between operator and agent as capabilities mature. That's the pendulum. And on the operator side, it climbs a ladder of management work the operator is still doing: physical labor, thumb-tax labor, and real attention. Past the assistant ladder, there's a second one, the agentic management ladder, with rungs from "doing it yourself" through "orchestrating delegation." The model's capability gets you to the next rung. Your trust, your harness, and your delegation maturity decide whether you can stand on it. A manager at a Burger King taught me this accidentally.

There is a darker layer beneath all of this. The smaller the bottleneck gets, the more dangerous it becomes. Micro-rituals (swipe, tap, continue, swipe, tap, continue) are individually too small to register as friction, so the operator absorbs them silently and adapts. By the second day of a smooth swipe-tap rhythm, the operator has stopped noticing they are doing it. That is not a workflow. That is the workflow training the operator. The Burger King manager has the same disease in mirror: none of her individual tasks were hard, and that is exactly why she never noticed she was the bottleneck.

The rest of this essay is her story, and ours.

## The Forty-Five-Minute Whopper

I placed the order online. An Impossible Whopper, lunch rush about to start. Walked into the store fifteen minutes later. I was the only customer in line.

That should have been the moment I picked up my food and left. It wasn't. The order wasn't ready. I stood at the counter and waited.

The lunch rush began. The line behind me grew. People filed in expecting to order, stood for a few minutes without anyone acknowledging them, and left. One after another. The store was getting busier in the kitchen and emptier at the counter at the same time.

The manager was doing every task herself. Taking what few orders did get placed. Assembling food. Checking the fryer. Calling names. Running drinks. Behind her, employees kept asking *what can I do to help?* and she could not stop long enough to answer. Not once. The question came, and she kept her head down on whatever task she was in the middle of, and the employee drifted back to the side of the kitchen to wait. Then a different employee asked. Same pattern. And another.

The walk-in customers had a choice. They left. The only people still in that dining room forty-five minutes after I'd placed the order were the ones who had ordered online: captives of a transaction that had already cleared, waiting for food we'd already paid for, with nowhere else to go.

She was working harder than anyone in the building. She was losing every customer who could leave. And the line at the counter was empty because no one was running it.

She wasn't short on capability or capacity. She was short on management. Specifically, delegation.

## The Move You See Coming

Everyone reading this is about to be that manager. Not because we're bad at our jobs. Because the capability showed up before the management maturity did.

Having an agent that *could* run a task unattended doesn't mean any of us has built the trust, the harness, or the instincts to let go. Having a team that *could* assemble the orders doesn't mean that manager had the instincts to delegate. The shape is the same.

So the question this essay is asking, and I want you to hold it as you read, is this: when you feel the friction of AI-augmented work, how sure are you that it's the model's fault?

A caveat before the frames. AI-augmented work is a much bigger country than assistants and agents. Search, retrieval, classification, generation, synthesis, embedded systems, background automation. Each has its own failure modes and its own ladder. I'm narrowing on purpose. The two frames that follow are about the operator's relationship to the thing doing the work, and assistants and agents are where that relationship is felt most directly. The rest of AI-augmented work deserves its own essays. This one stays here.

And one further caveat inside that scope. Even within assistants and agents, the delegation frame fits some tasks better than others. A one-shot translation, a quick code review, a single-turn synthesis. Those don't really live on the management ladder. They're transactions, not delegations. The ladder I'm about to describe is for the slice of work where the operator hands off something *ongoing*: a project, a feature, a workflow, where the question of trust accumulates over time. If your AI use lives mostly in one-shots, this essay is a frame to keep in your back pocket against the day it doesn't.

Two frames, side by side, and together they describe where the bottleneck actually lives.

## Frame One — The Pendulum

The bottleneck moves. It doesn't disappear.

A year ago the bottleneck was my hands. Every line of production code, typed. Every commit, mine. AI was for scaffolding, ideation, dictation. The bottleneck lived with the operator, and the operator was me.

Months ago the producing side had cheapened. Agents could draft. Agents could scaffold. Agents could rewrite. The reviewing side stayed on the operator, and the tax moved from "typing everything" to "reading everything the agent produced." Different bottleneck, same operator.

A few days ago, with Opus 4.7, the producing side collapsed into something that felt fluid for the first time. I wrote about that in *Fourteen Hours with Opus 4.7*, and the headline of that essay was exactly this: the producing got cheaper, the reviewing got more expensive, and the bottleneck slammed back onto the operator as supervision tax. Five pull requests for work that should have been two. An hour and forty minutes of broken production. A brilliant junior dev at the keyboard, producing work I couldn't trust without reading every line.

The composition took over fast. Bugbot catching issues the model missed. A Sonnet validator running the gauntlet against every PR. Managed agents absorbing scoped work end-to-end. Canon enforcing the gates between exploration, planning, and execution. The reviewing tax moved back onto the agent side, and my attention came back to me.

But then *this* week, six sessions into a well-oiled proactive loop, a new bottleneck showed up. One I hadn't predicted. The loop was rule-following, consistent, trustworthy. I was happy with the output. And my biggest job had become flipping back to the app every fifteen minutes to tap continue.

The tax had moved again. Not onto my judgment. Onto my thumb.

You see the pattern? The game isn't eliminating the bottleneck. It's choosing where it lives.

There is a darker reading of that same pattern, though, that I'll come back to. Each individual continue-tap costs almost nothing. Across a day that becomes hours of swipe-tap-continue, swipe-tap-continue, and at no point does any single act register as friction. So I don't flag it. So I keep doing it. So I build my day around it. The bottleneck isn't just on my thumb. The workflow has absorbed me as a mechanical component, and the rhythm is so small I almost missed it.

Hold that. The framework comes first.

## Frame Two — The Assistant Ladder

When I say *my attention is the bottleneck*, I'm collapsing three different things into one word. They deserve different names.

**Hands.** Physical labor. Typing, clicking, dragging, copy-pasting between tabs. The thing a year of agentic workflows has mostly removed from my life.

**Thumbs.** Residual physical labor that looks cognitive but isn't. Tapping continue. Approving a tool call. Flipping back to the app every fifteen minutes to say *yes, keep going*. This tier is sneaky in two ways. First, it feels like attention because you have to pay attention to *do* it. But the attention you're paying is to a button, not to a decision. You've dragged your judgment down to a mechanical act. Second, and this is the worse problem, the individual taps are too small to register as friction. So you don't flag them. So you adapt. So the rhythm becomes invisible. You stop noticing you're doing it. The day you stop noticing is the day the rhythm owns you.

**Attention.** Real judgment on real decisions. The irreducible thing. The part that should never be automated away because it's the part the operator is for.

The goal isn't eliminating the operator. The goal is climbing toward attention-only.

Ask yourself, honestly: how much of last week's AI-augmented work was your hands? How much was your thumbs? How much was your actual judgment on actual decisions?

If your answer is anything like mine, with the thumbs number higher than you'd like to admit, then you're somewhere I've been all month, and the rest of this essay is for both of us.

That was the assistant ladder. It describes what physical labor is still required when an AI helps you work. It's useful. It's not the whole truth.

## Frame Three — The Agentic Management Ladder

Assistants and agents are different animals, and they live on different ladders.

An assistant ladder asks: what physical labor is the operator still doing? An agentic ladder asks: what *management* labor is the operator still doing? That's a different axis. And the specific skill that lets you climb it is delegation.

**Doing it yourself.** No agent. You are the labor. Every line by hand. A year ago.

**Pair-programming.** Agent at the keyboard, you review every output line. Senior dev with a sharp junior driving. This is where most AI adoption starts and a lot of operators stall.

**Supervising tightly.** Scoped tasks, checkpoints at every step. Continue / approve / continue. This is the thumb-tax rung. This is where most operators are living right now with most models.

**Supervising loosely.** Defined task, end-to-end run, a couple of checkpoints. You read the result, not the intermediate steps.

**Delegating.** Goal in, outcome out. The Burger King manager who actually knew how to delegate, if she existed in that building, at that hour. I never saw her.

**Orchestrating delegation.** You manage agents that manage agents. The org chart of the AI org. The altitude a solo operator with the right harness might actually reach.

Each rung up is a delegation skill the operator has to earn. The model's capability gets you to the rung. The operator's trust, the harness's safety, and the canon's definition of *good at this altitude* decide whether the operator can actually stand on it.

This is the load-bearing frame. The rest of the essay is here.

## Why Operators Get Stuck On a Rung

Two failure modes. They look different from the inside, and they fail for opposite reasons.

**Staying too low.** The operator could climb but doesn't. Trust hasn't built. Or, more often, the *harness* hasn't built. Letting the agent run unattended requires governance the operator hasn't authored yet. Canon that says what *done* looks like without the operator watching. Validation that isn't the operator's eyeballs. The Burger King manager who keeps doing it herself because she can't trust the training, or the scripts, or the team's experience enough to step back. How much of last quarter's frustration came from work you could have handed off but didn't, because the scaffolding wasn't there yet?

**Climbing too fast.** The operator promotes the agent before the agent is ready. New hire made lead-on-shift day one. Agent runs solo, produces confident output, operator doesn't catch the failures until production. The *Fourteen Hours* sprawl was partly this: I trusted 4.7 to ship the feature without verifying the public interface myself, and the feature was broken for an hour and forty minutes. What was the last production failure you can name that came from climbing a rung before the harness could catch the fall?

The right pace is situational. It depends on the operator's delegation maturity, the model's behavior on *this specific kind of task* (not the model in general), and the harness you've built to catch the failure modes specific to *this rung*. Nothing about that is the model's job to solve. It's the operator's.

## Where 4.7 Actually Lives For Me, Right Now

Six sessions over the last few days on Opus 4.7, with the well-oiled loop. Handoff → orient → plan → PRD → gauntlet → execute → validate → merge. The coding and the reviewing were so well-oiled that my biggest job was flipping back to the app every fifteen minutes.

Continue. Approve. Continue.

The tool-call ceiling hit every fifteen minutes. The model stopped, produced a short summary of what was done and what was next, and waited for me to say *go on*. I tapped continue. It resumed. The cycle closed.

I could have automated it. I didn't. I was getting familiar with 4.7.

You don't promote a new hire to lead-on-shift on day one. You watch them work for a week. The thumb tax was the watching. It was the cost of building the trust I needed to climb to the next rung. That cost was worth paying once.

Paying it forever is the failure mode. And here is the part I almost missed entirely.

I lived two days of swipe, swipe, continue. Every fifteen minutes. The rhythm was so smooth that I described it as a well-oiled workflow. The rhythm was so small that I never flagged it as friction. And the rhythm was so constant that, by the second day, I had built my workday around it without noticing.

That is the real failure mode. Not the cost of any individual tap. The cost of adapting to a rhythm I should have refused. The Burger King manager has the same disease in mirror. None of her individual tasks were hard. Take an order. Run a drink. Check a fryer. Each one tiny. She rituals into doing all of them herself for almost two hours and never notices the ritual is the failure. She would have told you, asked at minute thirty, that she was being efficient.

I would have told you the same thing about my swipe-tap rhythm at minute thirty of day one. By minute thirty of day two I had stopped noticing the rhythm at all. That is not a workflow. That is the workflow training me.

And here is what the forcing function accidentally taught me. The shape of those ceiling-forced checkpoints (done, next, blocker or none, then wait) was exactly the shape every turn should have had from the start. Readable on a phone. Resumable in three seconds. Compressed. The *Fourteen Hours* postscript said we'd need to teach these models to *present*, to compress their hours of detection into our minutes of decision. The ceiling did that teaching without asking my permission.

The model wasn't failing. The format was failing. And I can fix the format without waiting for the next model release.

But the format fix is only half. The other half is refusing the ritual entirely. Larger turns. Pre-approved tool budgets within locked scope. The ceiling as the safety valve it was designed to be, not the heartbeat of the workflow. Anything that breaks the swipe-tap rhythm before I adapt to it is the right move.

That's the next rung, opening.

## Banning Questions in the Lunch Rush

The first time I ran Opus 4.7 it felt pedantic. Not wrong — pedantic. It asked questions. *Did you mean A or B? Should I include X? Which of these conventions applies?* Often. Sometimes mid-task. The *Fourteen Hours* postscript named this honestly: the questions weren't bad, they were catching real conflict between things I'd decided in different sessions. I just wasn't in the mood for them.

Of course I wasn't. The lunch rush was on.

Questions are a great thing. At the right time. When you are exploring possibilities, planning a path, or validating a result, questions are the work. They're how the system finds the cracks before they cost you. But in the middle of execution, when the order ticket is up and the fryer is running and the line is forming, twenty questions is not diligence. It's the manager doing every task herself with a different verb.

So I rewrote the harness to gate questions by mode.

In exploration: ask everything. The more questions the better. Surface every assumption.

In planning: ask everything that affects scope or approach. This is where ambiguity gets resolved. Every question asked here is a question that doesn't interrupt execution.

In execution: don't ask. If you have a question, look it up in the docs. Read the canon. Use your discernment and finish. The scope was set at the gate. Work it. If you genuinely cannot proceed without me, name the one specific blocker and revert to planning. But you don't get to ask twenty clarifying questions during execution. RTFM. Make the call.

In validation: float your concerns. All of them. This is where I want to hear what you noticed during execution that gave you pause. We decide together: accept, steer, or pivot. And then we loop back into execution with a new scope.

That single guidance change, questions encouraged in three modes and banned in the fourth, turned 4.7 from pedantic into the most pleasant coding I have ever done from my phone. No copy-paste rituals between tools. No handing context from one tab to another. Agents spawning to code. Agents spawning to validate. Both running. Me, mostly tapping continue between fifteen-minute turns because the tool-call ceiling kept hitting.

That was the bottleneck I needed.

I was no longer the bottleneck answering a dozen questions during every coding task. The model wasn't waiting for me to make decisions it could have looked up. The harness had taught it when to ask and when to read the manual, and the model, to its credit, took the training. The remaining bottleneck was a per-turn tool-call ceiling and my thumb.

That is what climbing a rung actually looks like, in detail, on a real day. No magic, no next-model-release dependency. A specific rule, written down, that moved a specific failure mode off the operator and onto a place where the agent could handle it itself. Multiply that rule by a hundred, over months, and you have a curriculum.

## What This Implies — The Limit Is Almost Never the Model

A scope honesty before I generalize. I am one operator. The ladder I've described rests mostly on my own work over the last year, and the upper rungs (loose supervision, full delegation, orchestrating delegation) I have only glimpsed in scoped runs, not lived in continuously. Treat the rungs as a working map drawn by a hiker who has walked the lower trail many times and seen the upper switchbacks from a distance. The shape is right. The footing of each upper rung will rhyme with mine and differ in particulars.

With that said: the ladder is the same for everyone. The rungs are the same. What differs is where you're standing today, and what's keeping you from climbing one more.

So ask yourself:

What physical labor are you still doing that could be thumb-tax? What thumb-tax are you still doing that could be pure attention? What attention are you still spending on tasks that, with the right canon and the right harness, could be delegated?

And — this is the one that matters — what's actually blocking the next rung for you? The model's capability? Or your trust? Or the harness you haven't built yet? Or something you haven't named because you haven't sat with it long enough?

My experience is that by the time an operator is feeling these costs, the model's capability is already past the rung they're standing on. The limit is almost never the model. The limit is the other three. And two of the three — the trust, the harness — belong to the operator, not to anyone shipping the next checkpoint.

If that lands uncomfortably, sit with it. The uncomfortable part is the interesting part.

## Nothing New Under the Sun, Still

The principles this essay is rediscovering are not new. Business schools have taught delegation, span of control, management maturity, and organizational design for a century. Good managers have built trust with their teams in measured steps. Bad managers have burned out doing every task themselves. That literature exists. Those principles travel.

The ladder I just walked you through has a name in that literature too. Hersey and Blanchard called it *situational leadership* in 1969: directing, coaching, supporting, delegating, with the right rung determined by the maturity of the person you're leading. The rungs I named map almost exactly onto theirs. I'm not claiming a discovery. I'm claiming an application.

This essay also has a parent in my own writing, and I want to name it directly because the lineage matters more than the cleverness of any single piece. In March I wrote an essay called *Copy. Paste.* about the ritual that had come to define AI-augmented work in 2025 and the first quarter of 2026: the highlight, the right-click, the tab swap, the paste, the back-back-paste again. I closed that essay with a specific wish. *I hope this essay ages poorly. I can't wait to not be able to imagine it.*

By April, the wish had largely come true *in my workflow*. The native-container coding experience I described in *Fourteen Hours with Opus 4.7* (an iOS chat that spawns its own coding environment, runs tests, ships features end-to-end without me carrying context across tabs) is exactly the workflow that ended the copy-paste ritual for me. The desk and mobile rituals that *Copy. Paste.* described as purgatory are not what my last week looked like.

The honest scope: my workflow. On the hardware and tools I actually use. The marketer pasting between ChatGPT and Google Docs is still in copy-paste purgatory. The consultant carrying AI output into a Notion board is still there. The ritual died where native containers reached. *Copy. Paste.* as a description still applies wherever they haven't. The wish came true in one workflow, on one schedule, and the broader community will get there at different speeds.

But notice what happened next, even in that one workflow. The very same tools that ended one ritual created a smaller one. The swipe-tap-continue rhythm I named in the *Where 4.7 Lives* section is the next instance of exactly the pattern *Copy. Paste.* was about. Same disease, smaller surface, harder to see. The micro-rituals are the smell. They were a smell when they were copy-paste between tools, and they are a smell now that they are continue-taps inside a native container.

What's new is applying the pattern to an org chart where one side is a model. What's also new, and worth saying out loud, is how *fast* the applications iterate. The copy-paste ritual defined the entire 2025 AI-augmented workflow. It died, in the workflows where it died, in roughly the time it took the relevant tools to ship one major release. The successor ritual was forming in the same workspace before the predecessor was cold. If you are reading this and the swipe-tap rhythm sounds insane to you, congratulations: by the time you read it, the workflow has probably already moved on. That is the right kind of aging poorly.

The operators who feel lost aren't lost. The map exists. *Nothing new under the sun.* The discipline sits on something durable underneath the iteration cycle, same as the last essay said.

What *is* genuinely new is the speed of the ladder. A rung that took a human manager five years to earn the right to stand on, an AI-native operator is being asked to earn the right to stand on in a quarter. That's a training problem for operators. Not a capability problem for models.

And if that's true, if the limiting factor is the operator's training and the harness they've authored, and not the model's next release, where does that put your investment? Your time? Your attention?

## Write First, Build Second

Here is the part I have been working on for two years, and the part I think most operators skip.

If the limiting factor is the operator's training and the harness they've authored, then the harness is the work. Not the model. Not the framework. Not the latest agentic platform. The harness is the written curriculum the agent learns to think inside of.

I built oddkit and the canon underneath it for exactly this reason. Write first. Build second. Before I asked an agent to make a decision on my behalf, I wrote down how I would make that decision and why. Before I asked it to validate something, I wrote down what *valid* means in the specific terms of this work. Before I asked it to encode a learning, I wrote down the shape a learning takes and the vocabulary it lives inside. The code came after the writing, every time. The writing *was* the system.

If you've ever raised a child or mentored someone junior, the move is familiar. You don't get to a teenager who makes good choices by behavior modification. You get there by explaining everything, for years, while the stakes are low. Why we do this. Why we don't do that. What good looks like. What you'd do in their place and why. The goal isn't compliance. The goal is graduating them from waiting for permission to being empowered to make the call themselves. The day they make a good call you didn't see coming is the day the curriculum worked.

Agents are not children. The analogy isn't the point. The *method* is the point. If you want to delegate, you write the curriculum first. You explain everything. You let the assistant ask the questions that show it doesn't yet think the way you think. You answer them, in writing, where the next agent (and the next, and the one after) can read what you said.

What this gave me, practically, is portability. The same canon and the same governance tooling work in Lovable, Codex, Cursor, Claude Code. The model behind each of those is doing better work with less steering, because the curriculum traveled with it. The harness isn't the IDE. The harness is what the IDE's model reads before it acts.

Ask yourself: how much of your delegation friction is the model not being smart enough? And how much is you having never written down how you actually make the call?

Because trust is a lot of hard work. And it is much easier to trust someone, or something, that thinks the way you do.

## Back to the Whopper

That manager didn't know she was the bottleneck. She thought she was being helpful. She thought she was holding the standard. She thought she was the only one who could do it right.

She probably was, at the level of any individual task. And the walk-in customers left. And the online orders sat captive, paid-for and stuck, because the people who'd already committed had nowhere to go.

That is the failure mode I want you to sit with. The work that can leave, leaves. The work that can't (your shipped commitments, your in-flight projects, the things you've already promised) sits in the dining room and waits. Forty-five minutes. An hour and forty minutes of broken production. Five pull requests for work that should have been two. The cost lands on the captives.

But there is a second failure mode running underneath the first one, and it's the one I almost missed. None of her individual tasks were hard. Each one was thirty seconds of work. The ritual of doing all of them herself never registered to her as effort. She would have told you, asked at minute thirty, that she was being efficient. By minute ninety she had stopped noticing the ritual at all. The ritual had become her workday.

That is the version of me that lived two days of swipe, swipe, continue. None of the taps was hard. The rhythm was so smooth I described it as a well-oiled workflow. By day two I had stopped noticing the rhythm. The workflow was training me.

The version of me that refuses to automate continue-taps because I don't quite trust 4.7 yet is the same manager at a smaller scale. Useful for a week, while the trust builds. Failure mode if it becomes permanent. The opportunities that could find another door, will. The commitments I've already made will sit and wait. And the rhythm of the ritual will get so quiet I will forget it is there at all.

The bottleneck moves. The ladder is real. The micro-rituals are the smell. The only question is whether I climb the ladder on purpose, refuse the rituals before they own me, and write the curriculum that lets the people, and the agents, around me do the same.
