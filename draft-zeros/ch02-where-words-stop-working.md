## Projected argument

Everyone has experienced the moment where more words make things worse. You're explaining something important — to a colleague, a child, a client — and you can see it's not landing. So you explain harder. More detail. More context. More examples. And with every additional sentence, you watch comprehension recede rather than advance.

This is cognitive saturation. The point where the listener's bandwidth is full and additional information becomes noise. It's not a failure of intelligence on either side. It's a structural limit of verbal knowledge transfer.

The frustration is universal: you *know* this thing, you can *feel* the shape of it, and language is failing you. The knowledge is multimodal — it has texture, weight, emotion, spatial relationships — and you're trying to force it through the narrow channel of sequential words.

Bible translation communities discovered this centuries ago. That's why oral translation uses acting, singing, dancing, embodying the text. Comprehension is multimodal. When words stop working, you need a different modality.

AI collaboration hits this same wall. You can write a longer prompt. You can add more context. But at some point, more specification produces worse output — because the AI is drowning in your instructions the same way your colleague was drowning in your explanation.

The solution in both domains is the same: stop talking and start checking. Transfer a small piece. Verify it landed. Adjust. Transfer the next piece. The cycle replaces the monologue.

Nothing new under the sun. The saturation point is the same whether you're explaining bedtime rules to a four-year-old or feeding context to an AI agent.
