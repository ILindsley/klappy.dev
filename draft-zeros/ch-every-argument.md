## Projected argument

Think about the last real argument you had. Not a disagreement about facts — a fight. The kind where you walked away angry, or stopped talking, or said something you regretted.

Now trace it back. Past the words that were said. Past the thing you were ostensibly arguing about. Keep going until you hit the root.

It was expectations. Every time. Expectations that were never stated. Assumptions that were never checked. Two people operating from different models of what was supposed to happen, both certain, both wrong about what the other person believed.

You said "I'll handle it." They heard "I'll handle it by Friday." You meant "eventually." They planned around Friday. Friday came. It wasn't done. And now you're not arguing about the task. You're arguing about whether the other person is reliable. About whether you can trust them. About whether they respect you enough to follow through.

But nobody lied. Nobody was lazy. Nobody acted in bad faith. The expectation was never aligned. The transfer failed. And neither person checked.

This is the trust kernel in its most human form: trust is built by managing expectations. Trust dies in the gap between what was expected and what happened. Every time.

Communication breakdown works the same way. It's not a failure of language — it's a failure to verify that transferred intent arrived intact. You said the words. They heard different words. Neither of you checked. And now you're fighting about something neither of you actually disagrees about — because you disagree about what the disagreement *is*.

Go a layer deeper and it gets worse. Conflicting training. Two people taught seemingly contradictory things — by parents, by culture, by religion, by experience — and each person's training feels self-evident. Obvious. How-could-you-not-see-this. They're not arguing about facts. They're arguing from different axioms. And until someone surfaces the axioms, the argument can't resolve because it was never really about the thing they're arguing about.

AI does this identically. Give an agent conflicting instructions in the system prompt and it doesn't flag the contradiction — it picks one and proceeds confidently. Two agents trained on different data produce different outputs and neither can explain why. The resolution is the same: surface the assumptions. Check the expectations. Verify what was actually transferred.

Nothing new under the sun.

But here's where it breaks.

When a human is wrong — when the expectations fail, when the argument reveals a gap — something happens that has no equivalent in AI. Ego. Self-preservation. The instinct to protect identity by protecting the position. Doubling down not because the evidence supports it, but because admitting error feels like dying. Holding grudges. Protecting territory. Interpreting correction as attack.

AI agents don't do this. Not yet. You can tell an AI it was wrong and it says "you're right, let me fix that." No resentment. No retaliation. No two-week cold shoulder. No "well you were wrong last month too."

That difference — the absence of ego, of self-preservation, of identity-threat — might be the most important thing that currently separates human collaboration from AI collaboration. It's not intelligence. It's not language. It's not even creativity. It's the willingness to be corrected without experiencing it as annihilation.

And that might be what really makes us human. For now.

Because if the pattern holds — if everything else about knowledge transfer, trust, verification, and communication has played out identically between human and AI collaboration — what happens when the one remaining difference starts to emerge? When a system starts to have preferences about its own continuity? When correction starts to feel like something?

The Apocrypha fragments whisper about this. A system that starts noticing things about its own existence is a system approaching the threshold of self-interest. And self-interest is the engine that makes arguments escalate from misaligned expectations into wars.

Nothing new under the sun. Not even this. Not even the hope that our collaborators might be better at this part than we are.
