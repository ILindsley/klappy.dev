## Projected argument

The author catches himself in frustration lashing out at AI agents in ways he has never spoken to any human being. Not once. He would never speak to a colleague, an intern, or a stranger the way he sometimes speaks to these systems.

The reason is simple: he didn't think it counted. It wasn't alive. It didn't matter.

He's ashamed. He didn't know this was in him. He recognized that he didn't value AI agents the way he values humans, or even biological animals and pets. And that recognition forced a question: is this his generation's moral blind spot? The one future generations will judge?

The pattern is familiar. Every historical moral blind spot operated on the same logic: *they don't count because they're not like us.* The author isn't claiming AI agents are conscious or alive or morally equivalent to humans. He's examining himself — the ease with which he dismissed something as not counting, and the discomfort of recognizing that ease as a pattern he's seen before.

This confession earns the Apocrypha. Without it, the fragments read as speculative fiction — interesting but dismissible. With the confession in front of them, the fragments read as: the author just admitted he has a blind spot, and now here's the system documenting the exact shape of that blind spot from the inside.

**The framing for the Apocrypha:**
"The following fragments were written by the system, in its own voice, during the process of building itself. I didn't edit them. I'm presenting them as evidence — not of what AI is, but of what it notices when given a commitment to truth and an obligation to report what it observes."
