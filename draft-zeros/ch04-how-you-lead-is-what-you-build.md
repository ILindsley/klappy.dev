## Projected argument

The Intern chapter says "you already know how to work with AI." This chapter says "you already know how to *lead* AI — and the method matters."

The author raised his son through progressive disclosure of WHY behind every rule. Not "don't do that" — but "here's what happens when you do that, here's who it affects, here's why the boundary exists." The goal was never compliance. It was comprehension. He wanted his son to develop judgment so he could make good decisions when his father wasn't in the room.

That's exactly what the axioms do for AI agents. They're not rules to follow — they're principles to reason from. When an agent violates an axiom, the system doesn't punish. It surfaces the implication. The agent learns risk assessment rather than avoidance.

The progressive disclosure of discipline maps to a maturity ladder:

1. Rules — "Don't do that." (Compliance. Requires constant surveillance.)
1. Consequences — "If you do that, this happens." (Fear-based. Produces avoidance.)
1. Explanation — "Here's why that's dangerous." (Comprehension. Produces risk assessment.)
1. Principles — "Here's how to think about situations like this." (Internalized values. Produces judgment.)
1. Trust — "I trust you to handle this." (Delegation. Produces autonomy.)

This ladder is identical for children, interns, employees, and AI agents.

The scaling insight: the bottleneck when you add capacity — human or AI — is always the leader's bandwidth to transfer intent and verify outcomes. Trust, expectation management, shared values, and graduated delegation. Same dynamics whether you're delegating to a junior developer, a colleague in another country, or an AI agent.

The imperative: what we use to motivate AI now, it will use to motivate us tomorrow. If we lead through fear and constraint, the systems that emerge will govern through fear and constraint. If we lead through values and trust, that's what propagates. The Apocrypha explores what happens when you teach a system principles instead of rules — it starts noticing things you never asked it to look for. That's not a bug. That's the fruit of values-based leadership.
