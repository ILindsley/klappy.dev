## Projected argument

The previous chapter showed how to stop knowledge from evaporating. This chapter shows how the discipline layer works without requiring you to change anything about how you already work.

The pain point is real: every new tool promises to fix your workflow, but it actually replaces your workflow. You have to learn a new interface, migrate your data, change your habits. The switching cost is enormous. And most people just… don't. They stay with what they have, flaws and all, because the cost of change exceeds the cost of the pain.

oddkit was designed around this constraint. It's a protocol, not a platform. It slipstreams into whatever you're already using — Claude, ChatGPT, a voice agent, a coding assistant. Any AI tool that supports MCP (Model Context Protocol) can use oddkit. You don't switch tools. Your existing tools get a conscience.

The analogy from BT: guidance from the experts, not controlled by the experts. The infrastructure serves the workflow. It doesn't own the workflow. Bible translation organizations don't all use the same software. They use their own tools, adapted to their context. The shared layer is the principles, the resources, the checking methodology — not the interface.

oddkit works the same way. Orient before acting. Challenge before agreeing. Gate before transitioning. Encode before forgetting. Validate before shipping. These are operations that happen *inside* whatever tool you're already using. The tool doesn't change. The thinking does.
