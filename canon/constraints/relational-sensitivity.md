# Relational Sensitivity — Truth Told in Love

> The author works closely with the organizations and individuals referenced in public essays. Truth is told honestly but framed positively — the pattern is structural, not personal. Nobody is the villain. Organizations aren't broken; they're doing the hard work of coordinating humans, which is relational and political at the same time. The insight in any essay is recognizing patterns, not assigning blame. This constraint derives from the trust kernel: the author's ongoing relationships with colleagues, partners, and organizations are trust assets that the writings must not damage. Honesty without love is cruelty. Love without honesty is flattery. Both betray trust.

-----

## Summary — Relationships Are Trust Assets the Writings Must Not Damage

Public essays draw from real projects, real teams, and real conversations. The author maintains active working relationships with the people and organizations in these stories. This creates a constraint that governs all public writings: truth must be told, but it must be told in a way that honors the relationships it could affect.

This is not a marketing posture or a diplomacy strategy. It is an application of the trust kernel. Every relationship the author has with a colleague, partner, or organization is a trust asset built through managed expectations. A public essay that frames someone as a villain — even accurately — damages that trust in a way that cannot be repaired by being right.

The test is not "is this true?" The test is "would the person described recognize themselves in this and feel respected?"

-----

## The Five Constraints

**1. Frame organizational friction as structural, not personal.**
Organizations have planning cycles, stakeholder alignment processes, and coordination overhead. These are structural realities, not failures of leadership. When an essay describes a project that spent a year planning before building, the framing is "the planning had peaked" — not "the team wasted a year."

**2. Use positive framing — show what worked, not what was wrong.**
The shelf translation section in *The Voice Came First* models this: lead with what oral-first approaches produced, not with what expert-first approaches got wrong. The reader arrives at the contrast through the positive evidence, not through accusation.

**3. Honor both the relational and political tensions in organizations.**
The relational work builds trust. The political work builds alignment. Both are necessary. Both slow things down. Sometimes the care that makes an organization trustworthy is the same thing that keeps it from building. Name both sides. Don't collapse one into the other.

**4. When sensitivity requires pseudonyms or geographic generalization, apply them consistently.**
Record the substitution map in internal documents (not public). Apply pseudonyms uniformly across all public references to the same entity. Sensitivity decisions are made per the request of the people involved, not unilaterally by the author.

**5. Shared confession, not finger-pointing.**
The posture of *The Broken Wall and the Buried Talent* applies everywhere: "we" not "they." The author is part of the ecosystem being described. The failures are shared. The insights are offered, not imposed.

## Provenance — Where This Constraint Was Observed

This constraint was applied informally across three published essays before being encoded:

- **Seven Weeks to Southeast Asia** — Pseudonyms for minority language communities and named individuals per Teryl's explicit request. Geographic generalization (Laos → Southeast Asia). Substitution map recorded in internal case study.
- **Where Words Stop Working** — Relational/political tension paragraph: "The relational work builds trust. The political work builds alignment. Both are necessary. Both slow things down." Sensitivity constraint embedded in source material from the start.
- **The Broken Wall and the Buried Talent** — Yvonne's quotes verified by speaker before publication. Colleagues anonymized. Tone converted from accusation to shared confession across 22+ revision rounds.

The pattern was consistent enough across three independent essays to warrant formal encoding. The constraint is permanent and applies to all future public writings.

## Connection to Writing Canon

This constraint extends the Writing Canon checklist. Before committing any public writing, add this check:

**Would the people and organizations described recognize themselves in this essay and feel respected?** If not, the framing needs work — not the truth. The facts don't change. The posture does.
